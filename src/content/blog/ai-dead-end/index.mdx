In November 2025, a researcher walked into Mark Zuckerberg's office and quit. Not just any researcher—a winner of the 2018 Turing Award for the breakthroughs that made modern deep learning possible. His name is **Yann LeCun**, and if anyone knows AI, it's him.

<Callout title="Who is Yann LeCun?">
  A French-American computer scientist, Chief AI Scientist at Meta (2013–2025),
  and professor at NYU. He is best known for pioneering **convolutional neural
  networks (CNNs)** in the late 1980s—the architecture that powers most modern
  computer vision systems. Alongside Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio, he is
  considered one of the three "Godfathers of Deep Learning."
</Callout>

<Callout title="What is the 2018 Turing Award?">
  The Turing Award is widely regarded as the "Nobel Prize of computing," given
  annually by the ACM for major contributions to computer science. In 2018, it
  was jointly awarded to LeCun, Hinton, and Bengio for the conceptual and
  engineering breakthroughs that made deep neural networks a critical component
  of computing.
</Callout>

Four months later, he had raised more than a billion dollars to build something designed to prove the entire industry wrong.

LeCun's pitch to investors: **large language models are dead.** Not useless—but as a path to human-level intelligence, a dead end.

The pattern of the last few years tells the story. Big tech keeps throwing more compute at LLMs and getting diminishing returns. At the end of the day, these are token prediction machines. They aren't going to turn into AGI on their own, and they still can't get the basics right.

<Callout title="What is AGI?">
  **Artificial General Intelligence** refers to a hypothetical AI that can
  understand, learn, and perform any intellectual task a human can—across any
  domain, without being narrowly trained for it. Today's LLMs are considered
  "narrow AI": impressive at language tasks, but lacking the flexible, grounded
  understanding humans have.
</Callout>

LeCun is **not saying AI is useless**. He uses it every day and is positive about Claude and ChatGPT. His claim is narrower:

- We've exhausted most of the output we're going to get from LLMs
- If our goal is human-level intelligence, scaling LLMs will never get you there
- It won't be solved with more parameters, more GPUs, or more data centers
- **The architecture itself has hit a wall**

## Four walls LLMs can't climb

**1. No model of the physical world.** An LLM can describe the world in dozens of languages, but it has never been to Paris, seen a waterfall, or touched a puppy. Gravity, friction, heat, hunger—these are just tokens, with no innate meaning behind them. **A 4-month-old baby has a better physics engine than GPT-5.** Human intelligence is much more than predicting the next word in a sentence.

<Callout title="LeCun's alternative: JEPA">
  Instead of LLMs, LeCun champions the **Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture
  (JEPA)**—a model that learns by predicting abstract representations of the
  world from sensory input (video, images), rather than predicting tokens. The
  aim is to give machines an intuitive "world model" similar to the one infants
  develop.
</Callout>

**2. No persistent memory.** Every interaction starts from zero. What looks like memory is just the chatbot stuffing prior turns back into the prompt each time—a notebook, not a mind. Context windows are a goldfish with a very good notebook. Human memory is an elephant that never forgets.

**3. No real ability to reason.** LLMs guess at information rather than retrieve it. When they appear to reason, they're pattern-matching against reasoning traces from their training data—Reddit threads, textbook solutions, Stack Overflow answers. That's why an LLM can solve a hard math problem straight from a textbook yet fail on something trivial a child handles easily.

The human difference runs deeper than capability. Humans carry cultural values, social norms, and a built-in sense of what helps or hurts them. An LLM has no skin in the game—because there's no skin. It predicts what the right answer might _look like_, not what it actually is.

<PullQuote>
  "What's most important to being human is not ever written down."
</PullQuote>

**4. Limited planning.** Ask an LLM to plan your week and it will produce something that _looks_ like a plan—but it isn't simulating the future. It's stitching together patterns from weeks it has seen before. Show it something new and the seams split: a meeting on one side of the city followed immediately by another on the opposite side, with no sense of geography or travel time. Plausible, rarely useful.

Language, LeCun argues, is a **thin, compressed shadow of reality**. Training on text alone is like **learning to swim by reading a textbook**.

<Callout title='The "stochastic parrot" critique'>
  LeCun's view echoes a 2021 paper by Emily Bender and Timnit Gebru that called
  LLMs "stochastic parrots"—systems that stitch together linguistic forms based
  on probability without any understanding of meaning. LeCun's argument extends
  this: no amount of scaling fixes the missing grounding in physical reality.
</Callout>

## So what?

If LeCun is right, the hundreds of billions being poured into data centers, GPUs, and power stations right now are funding the final chapter of a dead-end technology.

That doesn't mean LLMs disappear. For some workflows—**coding especially**—they're genuinely valuable. But the hype about autonomous agents replacing knowledge work will pass. Enormous capital has already been deployed, and outside coding the technology has yet to deliver real economic value.

## The vibe coding paradox

Even in coding—LLMs' best domain—the hype has cratered. In November 2025, Collins Dictionary named **"vibe coding"** its Word of the Year. Six months later, the same LinkedIn crowd that hyped it is now generating articles about how vibe coding is dead.

The promise: vibe coding would finally deprecate "those weird geeks with no social skills and high-paying jobs," turn project managers into millionaire solopreneurs, and let companies cut human costs. The reality: non-engineers shipping bad software and discovering that skipping the engineering part of software engineering produces software that doesn't work.

<Callout title="What is vibe coding?">
  Coined by Andrej Karpathy in early 2025, **vibe coding** is the practice of
  building software by describing what you want to an LLM in natural language
  and accepting whatever it generates—without reading the code, understanding
  the architecture, or doing traditional engineering review.
</Callout>

The hype isn't going away—it's being renamed. **"Vibe engineering"** and **"agentic engineering"** are the next iterations. Brace yourself for the revelation: agentic engineering is software engineering. The kind practiced since the 1960s.

The "revolutionary" practices being rediscovered:

1. **Write a specification.** Decide what the thing is supposed to do before you build it.
2. **Task decomposition.** Break the work into smaller, well-defined tasks. Covered in the first two weeks of any CS degree.
3. **Code review.** Thoroughly review the code before merging. If the review-and-reprompt loop takes longer than writing it yourself, write it yourself.

<PullQuote>
  We are slowly going back to building software like we did a couple of years
  ago, but framing it as a completely new thing.
</PullQuote>

This isn't the first time the industry has tried to democratize programming by making the syntax friendlier. When **Grace Hopper** and her peers designed **COBOL** in 1959, the explicit goal was to let managers and non-programmers read and write code in English-like syntax.

COBOL succeeded as infrastructure—it still runs much of global finance. But it **failed Hopper's actual goal**. Rather than empowering non-technical managers to build complex systems, it forced programmers to write extremely verbose, rigid, hard-to-maintain code. When the **Y2K bug** hit, the business managers who were supposed to maintain it couldn't handle the technical debt, and retired COBOL programmers were dragged back into the workforce at premium rates.

Plain-English programming has been tried. It doesn't remove the need for engineers—it just defers the bill.

<Callout title="The tokenomics irony">
  Plain English was supposed to make software development more accessible. But
  pay-per-token pricing is now compressing prompts back into pidgin: "make
  button blue" beats "please modify the visual appearance of the aforementioned
  interactive element." If you find yourself typing "update object, no fluff,
  just code, make no mistakes," you've reinvented an inefficient, expensive
  programming language.
</Callout>

Even in coding, where LLMs genuinely help, the economics are not what the hype suggested. As writing code gets cheaper, **everything downstream gets more expensive**:

- Higher code churn—large volumes of generated code are quickly rewritten, reverted, or patched
- Developers increasingly act as reviewers, integrators, and debuggers
- Less time spent typing; more effort spent on validation, debugging, and long-term maintenance

**The real cost lies in judgment, not generation.** And judgment is where technical skill matters most. On the surface, "developer using AI" and "non-technical person vibe coding" can look identical. The outputs are not.

## Closing thought

LeCun might be wrong. He has been before—he was skeptical of transformers early on, and transformers went on to power the very LLMs he now critiques. But betting against a Turing Award winner who walked away from a billion-dollar role at Meta to build the alternative? That's a bet worth thinking carefully about.

If "vibe coding" was the word of 2025, expect **"plateauing"** to be the word of 2026.
